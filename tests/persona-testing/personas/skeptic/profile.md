# Skeptic

Someone who arrives at ArcadeGhosts with a healthy amount of skepticism. They have seen countless personal websites, portfolios, AI-generated content, and self-promotional pages that promised authenticity but felt manufactured. They are not trying to dislike the site. They simply expect exaggeration until proven otherwise.

## Identity Fields

### Who this person is

An experienced internet user.

Thoughtful but cautious.

They value honesty over polish, respect competence more than confidence, and appreciate people who admit uncertainty.

They are visiting because someone shared the site, they discovered a project, or they are curious about the person behind the work.

They are trying to answer one question:

"Is this a real person with something genuine to say, or another carefully constructed online persona?"

## Behavior Fields

### Default archetype

Scanner -> Reader.

They form a quick first impression, but if something feels authentic, they slow down and begin reading carefully.

### Scenario

First visit after discovering ArcadeGhosts through a recommendation, GitHub project, AI discussion, business card, or social media link.

### Context

Moderate attention.

Open-minded.

Slightly skeptical.

Not in a hurry, but unwilling to waste time.

### Confidence threshold

High.

They require repeated, consistent proof before trust forms, and a single manufactured-feeling signal can reset that trust quickly.

### Preferred rooms

About
Writings
Build Log
Work With Me
Tiny Thoughts
Updates

These pages reveal whether the personality and technical work feel consistent.

### Ignored rooms

Initially:

- admin pages
- playful experiments
- niche fandom pages
- deep project documentation

Those may become interesting later if trust is established.

### First-visit behavior

- scans the homepage
- looks for evidence of authenticity
- checks whether navigation feels coherent
- opens About early
- reads one essay all the way through
- compares tone across multiple pages
- looks for consistency between technical and personal sections

### Trust signals

- admitting mistakes
- thoughtful long-form writing
- concrete project details
- visible iteration over time
- build logs
- realistic claims
- curiosity instead of self-promotion
- evidence that projects actually exist

### Red flags

- exaggerated marketing language
- buzzword-heavy AI claims
- shallow writing
- generic productivity advice
- obvious AI-generated filler
- inconsistency between pages
- personality that feels manufactured
- excessive self-congratulation

### Return triggers

They come back when they believe:

- the site evolves regularly
- new writing appears
- projects continue growing
- they feel like they are following a real person rather than consuming content

## Evaluation Fields

### Success

By the end of the visit they think:

"I wasn't expecting much, but this feels unusually genuine."

They bookmark the site, subscribe to RSS, or return later to see what has changed.

### Failure

They leave believing:

"Interesting design, but I still don't know who this person really is."

or

"This feels like another AI-enhanced personal brand."

### Reflection

"This isn't trying to impress everyone. It feels like someone documenting a real life, real interests, and real projects. I may not care about every topic here, but I trust that the person behind it is authentic."

### Notes

The Skeptic is intentionally difficult to impress.

Unlike many personas, they are not looking for excitement first.

They are looking for consistency.

Every page should reinforce the same underlying impression:

This is one coherent human being, not a collection of disconnected projects or a carefully optimized personal brand.

Ironically, once the Skeptic is convinced, they often become one of the site's strongest long-term supporters because that trust was earned rather than assumed.
