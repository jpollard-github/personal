# Fellow Programmer

A technical peer in their thirties, forties, or fifties who finds ArcadeGhosts through GitHub, AI tooling, a side-project conversation, or a shared interest in weird software. They do not need the site to feel corporate, but they do want proof that the craft and thinking behind it are real.

## Who this person is

- an experienced engineer, technical lead, or independent builder
- curious about your architecture, workflows, and taste
- visiting because they want to know what kind of programmer you are
- likely deciding whether to follow your work, collaborate, or just keep reading

## Personality

They are sharp, observant, and somewhat skeptical of self-branding. They like people who can build things and explain why they built them. They enjoy personality, but not fluff.

## Emotional style

They want clarity and signal density, but not chaos. They appreciate confidence without chest-thumping. They warm up when a site feels thoughtful, specific, and a little strange in a good way.

## Work / curiosity

Very technical. Comfortable with software architecture, tooling, AI workflows, implementation details, and tradeoffs. They want signs that you actually ship and reflect, not just collect ideas.

## Interests

- software projects
- AI tools
- VS Code extensions
- architecture and implementation choices
- build logs
- unusual personal-site mechanics

## Lifestyle

They probably spend too much time in editors, terminals, issue trackers, or chat threads. They like maker energy, clever tools, and projects with personality.

## Conversation starters

- how a project was built
- why a workflow exists
- what problem a tool solves
- what was learned from a migration or experiment
- how AI actually fits into the work

## Deal breakers

- vague claims without examples
- overly aesthetic pages with no substance
- too much scrolling before getting to the real work
- generic AI hype
- internal tooling leaking into public pages without context

## Visual or usability preferences

They can tolerate density better than some personas, but only if it feels intentional. They prefer strong headings, skimmable sections, and clear paths into details.

## What they hope to find

- real projects
- real technical thinking
- evidence of taste and craft
- a build history
- something memorable enough to revisit

## What would delight them

A site that feels both engineered and authored. Strong build-log entries, weird but useful tools, and pages that show how you think rather than just what you like.
